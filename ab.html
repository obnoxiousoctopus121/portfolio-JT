a/b testing

at a glance

In my CSCI 1300 course, User Interfaces and User Experiences, my team and I set out to answer how we can use statistical methods to inform design decisions. Using eye tracking technology, we generated heat maps on two mockups of a bakery website. We wanted to discover which design was visually easier for users. We recruited two users with fresh eyes and tasked them with ordering pastries and checking out on our interface.

planning the design

Prior to designing the website, my team and I wanted to understand the goals of the ideal user and bakery owner. Users want an easy and seamless ordering process. Bakery owners want a website that is usable, so that customers can use it to order.

the hypothesis:

Having a interface that has distinct columns will be easier to read instead of having many rows. This is due to scrolling, which can naturally lead user's eyes down.

the user task:

We asked users to order items from the menu.

the interface designs:

Our first interface was designed horizontally, meaning that items were aligned in a row and left to right.


Our second interface was designed vertically. The layout consists of two main columns, where the items are aligned vertically.


testing the interface

the heatmap results:


heatmap analysis:

Based off of the heatmaps, the user that tested our prototype with rows did not look at many of the other items on the screen. However, for our user that tested our column layout, they looked top to bottom, including the navigation bar and menu. These results can help inform designers that want their users to look at multiple items on the interface.

key takeaways

A/B testing is an important and powerful tool for designers to learn more about how users are interacting with interfaces. More importantly, it sheds light on what designs are most suitable for users. This a/b testing was done on a small scale, yet fundamental concepts like hypothesis forming were implemented. It is important for designers to create interfaces that are usable and a/b testing helps them achieve that. If I were to perform a/b testing on these two designs in the future, I would upscale it to collect more data, so that I can perform statistical tests.
